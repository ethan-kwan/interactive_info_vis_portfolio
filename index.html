Context: This sketch is an ambient kitchen timer that merges an analog clock with the body of a kettle. It highlights how kettles usually take a few minutes to boil, and makes that waiting time visible and a bit more playful for someone brewing tea or coffee at the stove.

  • Design decisions: I turned the kettle into a clock face so the user can read time directly from the object they are waiting on. A visible fire under the kettle reinforces that the water is actively heating, and the second hand tracks real-world time so the user can see exactly how many seconds and minutes have passed.
  • Design decisions: Each time a full minute passes (as indicated by the seconds hand completing a full rotation), an extra burst of steam comes out of the spout. This ties a familiar visual cue—steam—with the more abstract notion of elapsed minutes, making the passage of time easier to feel and not just read.
  • Future work: I would like to improve the visuals with more detailed shading, textures, and smoother steam motion, and potentially add a start/stop button that lets the user “turn on” the heat so the clock and steam only begin once they intentionally start the boil.

HWK 5 — Visualization notes

Why this dataset & story: I chose the NBA player index because it is familiar, rich, and already organized at the player level. Height and scoring are intuitive metrics for basketball fans, so “career points per game by height” becomes a straightforward and interesting question: do taller players actually score more over their careers, or is that just a stereotype?

Title & framing: The title “The taller the better? 🏀” is intentionally a little provocative while still clearly hinting at the comparison. It invites the viewer to test a common belief rather than just stating the answer up front.

Narrative & genre: This is mostly author‑driven: I choose the bins, the minimum seasons filter, and the framing question, while the user explores by visually comparing bars. The visualization is an infographic‑style bar chart, with storytelling elements like a highlighted bar, explanatory subtitle, and small annotations for sample size.

How to read it: Each bar shows the average career points per game for players whose height falls within a 2‑inch bin, limited to players who have played at least a minimum number of seasons. The highlighted bar marks the height range with the highest average PPG, and the n= labels show how many players contribute to each bin so you can judge which averages are more stable.

Design principles: The main priorities were comparability, stability, and trust. I used simple vertical bars on a shared baseline so height groups are easy to compare, chose 2‑inch bins to balance detail and noise, filtered to multi‑season careers so one‑year outliers do not dominate, and surfaced sample size directly in the chart. A warm highlight color on the peak bin and lighter gridlines create a clear visual hierarchy.

Clarity, hierarchy, memorability: Clarity comes from a single main mapping (height bins on the x‑axis, average career PPG on the y‑axis) and a title/subtitle that restate that mapping in plain language. Hierarchy is guided by visual weight: the title and highlighted bar draw attention first, followed by axis labels and then footnotes. Memorability comes from tying the chart to a question fans already ask (“do taller players really score more?”) and from labeling height ranges in familiar feet‑and‑inches notation.
